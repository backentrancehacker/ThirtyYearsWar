30 Years' War

Nathan Pham

1555

The Peace of Augsburg concluded the Reformation. The Holy Roman Empereror could not enforce a unified religion, and the state princes could choose Catholicism or Lutheranism in their land. Calvinism was illegal, and its rapid spread threatened the Empire.

1618

The Habsburgs wrote a Letter of Majesty guaranteeing the Bohemians the right to practice their Protestant religions. However, Ferdinand II revoked the letter, which increased religious tensions.

1618 (Bohemian Phase)

In response, the Bohemian nobility threw Ferdinand's representatives out of Prague Castle; this became known as the Defenestration of Prague. The emissionaries somehow survived by landing on a mound of dung, and Ferdinand crushed the rebellion in 1620.

1624

Denmark-Norway's King Christian IV entered the Thirty Year's War to support the Protestants in Germany. He sought to limit the political power of other kings and defend against the expansion of Catholicism.

1629 (Danish Phase)

The Lutheran King of Denmark allied with the Lutheran princes to invade the Holy Roman Empire. They failed to penetrate the Imperial army and fled. In 1629 Ferdinand issued the Edict of Restitution, ordering Protestants to relinguish their land originally owned by Catholics.

1631 (Swedish Phase)

The Lutheran King of Sweden, Gustavus Adolphus, invaded the Holy Roman Empire and reclaimed the land taken from the Protestant Union. Adolphus introduced modern warefare and mobile artillery, but he was still killed in the Battle of Lutzen in 1632.

1635 (French Phase)

Cardinal Richeliu, the First Minister of France joined the conflict and financially supported the Protestant Swedes. He was more interested in attacking the Habsburgs than Europe's religious affiliations.

1643

Denmark-Norway allied with the Holy Roman Empire and 5-year-old Louis XIV ascended to the French throne. The following years drew the conclusion of the war, and in 1647 the Swedes captured Prague Castle in the Battle of Prague.

1648 (End of War)

Various participants of the war signed several treaties that became known as the Peace of Westphalia, ending the Thirty Years' War. It weakened the Holy Roman Emperor, rendered the Netherlands and Switzerland independent of the Habsburgs, and officially recognzed Calvinism.
